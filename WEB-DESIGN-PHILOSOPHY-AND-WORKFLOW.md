# Web Design Philosophy & Client Engagement Workflow
*Distilled from 20-80 Solutions' published process, their intranet-linked SOPs, and the July 2026 automation planning work. Written to be reusable on any client-services web project — the healthcare specifics are called out so you can swap them for another industry's rules.*

---

## 1. The Philosophy

### 1.1 The Pareto core: 20% of effort → 80% of results
Every decision filters through one question: **is this in the 20% that actually moves the outcome?**
- Don't build 40 pages when 14 pages targeted at money keywords do the work.
- Don't offer infinite design options; offer a proven structure with the client's uniqueness layered on top.
- Don't report 30 metrics; report the 3 the owner acts on (enquiries, cost per enquiry, return per $1).

### 1.2 Differentiation-first, template-second
The site structure, page types and conversion patterns are **templated** — they're solved problems.
What is *never* templated is **what makes this business unique**. The workflow's most important step is extracting the differentiators (e.g. "only clinic in the suburb with IV sedation", "Mandarin-speaking hygienist", "25 years in Blackburn") and making them the spine of the copy, the headlines and the ad angles.

> Rule of thumb: a visitor should be able to say what makes this business different within 10 seconds of landing — without scrolling.

### 1.3 Design + content in ONE pass
Design and content are not sequential phases. Templated, industry-proven content is infused with the client's philosophy and differentiators, and the design is applied around it **in a single pass**. This kills the classic agency deadlock ("waiting on copy" / "waiting on design").

### 1.4 SEO comes AFTER the site is complete
The website must be finished — structure, content, differentiators — before SEO begins. SEO applied to an unfinished site is wasted effort. Order: **build → complete → metadata/technical SEO → local SEO (GBP, citations) → monitoring baseline**.

### 1.5 Compliance is a hard gate, not a review step
In healthcare this is AHPRA: no testimonials, no outcome guarantees, no misleading before/afters. The lesson generalises: **codify the industry's rules and enforce them at the point of writing**, not in a late review. Surface constraints during onboarding (when the client asks for testimonials, explain the rule *at that moment* and offer the compliant alternative).

### 1.6 Facts live in one place
Opening hours, team lists, fees, booking links — each fact is entered once and propagates everywhere (site pages, footer, schema, Google Business Profile). Clients never need to know which pages mention their hours.

### 1.7 Plain language, always
Client-facing words are in patient-and-dollar language, never marketing jargon.
- ✅ "Your website brought 41 new patient enquiries. Every $1 on ads returned $10.90."
- ❌ "CTR improved 12% with a CPL delta of −$4.10."
House style for bad news: **state it + pair it with the action already taken.** *"One ranking dipped — we found the cause the same morning and it's already recovering."*

### 1.8 Trust structure over lock-in
- No lock-in contracts. 6-month money-back guarantee.
- Face-to-face (or at least face-on-video) relationships, especially at kickoff and reviews.
- The guarantee is a forcing function: the process must actually work.

## 2. Client Engagement Playbook

### 2.1 The funnel that wins the work
1. **Free audit as the hook** — a genuinely useful "online presence audit" of their current site/rankings/reviews. Give value before asking for anything.
2. **Face-to-face consult** — walk the audit; listen more than pitch. Goal of meeting #1: understand what makes them different, not close.
3. **Differentiation conversation** — "what do patients/customers say when they recommend you?" This is where the project is actually won.
4. **Proposal anchored to ROI** — talk in their numbers (patient value × enquiries), not in deliverables lists.

### 2.2 Onboarding: the 10-question intake
One structured questionnaire (form or wizard), completable in ~25 minutes, save-anywhere, mobile-friendly:

| # | Ask | Why |
|---|-----|-----|
| 1 | Practice/business details | facts layer, compliance registration |
| 2 | **What makes you unique** | the differentiators — the most important answer; a strategist reviews it live with them |
| 3 | Services | page map |
| 4 | Ideal customers/patients | tone + targeting |
| 5 | Target suburbs/areas | local SEO spine, money keywords |
| 6 | Preferred providers/funds/partners | trust signals |
| 7 | Booking links & operational software | integrations |
| 8 | Design preferences (from a gallery) | constrain taste debates early |
| 9 | 2–3 websites they admire, and why | decode taste into requirements |
| 10 | Logo & brand assets upload | asset chase starts immediately if missing |

**Rules:**
- Missing assets never block progress — chase in a fixed sequence (+2d email, +5d email, +7d phone call).
- Contradictions get surfaced, not silently resolved (e.g. "premium positioning" + "compete on price" → a question for the kickoff, not a coin flip).
- Check conflicts of interest (same suburb, same specialty as an existing client) **before** kickoff.

### 2.3 The brief (PRD) — merge client input with your codified knowledge
The intake answers merge with your versioned knowledge documents into one build brief:

```
PRD skeleton                          [source]
1. Identity & differentiators         [intake Q1–2 + strategist pass]
2. Services & page map                [Q3 × your page-priority rules]
3. Target customers & tone            [Q4 × tone matrix]
4. Local SEO plan + money keywords    [Q5 × your SEO rules]
5. Compliance constraints (HARD)      [industry rulebook, versioned]
6. Design direction                   [Q8–9 × your design principles]
7. Integrations                       [Q7]
8. Acceptance checklist               [your SOP library]
```

**The human 20%:** a strategist always reviews the brief before build — their job is to sharpen the differentiators. Everything else can be assisted/automated; this step can't.

### 2.4 Communication rules during the build
- Every request, from any channel (email/phone/portal), lands in **one tracked list** with a status the client can see.
- Acknowledge within 1 business hour: what we understood, the ETA, anything we still need.
- When work completes, tell them **the moment it's done**, with a link to see it. The "it's done" message is the single cheapest loyalty builder in the industry.
- Client-owned action items get polite automatic chasing (+5d, +10d) — most stalled projects are stalled waiting on the client, and silence reads as agency slowness.
- Never hide bad news; always pair it with the action taken.

## 3. The Build Workflow (≈ 9 weeks, published to the client)

Publish this to the client as a visual timeline — it sets expectations and shows exactly where their approvals and inputs sit. Lanes: **Client** / **Agency** / **Internal**.

### Week 1–2 · Kickoff
| Client | Agency |
|---|---|
| Complete questionnaire · kickoff meeting · initial deposit · send credentials, assets & logo | Create project workspace · internal kickoff · information architecture |

### Week 3–4 · Design & content (one pass)
- Select theme/design direction; write copy from templates + differentiators.
- Optional: photo/video shoot, brand creation, email (Google Workspace) setup.
- **Client approval gate #1** — direction sign-off.

### Week 5–6 · Build
- Develop on staging · populate content & changes · internal peer review of unique pages (the differentiator pages get the scrutiny — templated pages are already proven).

### Week 7–8 · Launch
- **Client approval gate #2** — final sign-off.
- Production testing → go live.
- Optional: Google Business Profile setup, Google Ads setup.

### Week 9+ · SEO & handover
- Internal SEO (metadata for every page — titles ≤ 60 chars, descriptions ≤ 155, unique across the site, compliance-checked).
- External/local SEO (GBP, citations, money keywords registered in rank tracking).
- Training/handover · final invoice · debrief · **satisfaction survey** · sign-up to ongoing support.
- New site enters a **14-day monitoring baseline** (no alarm-raising while rankings settle).

**Workflow discipline that makes this run:**
- Stage transitions fire on *events* (form done, approval clicked, deploy finished) — not on someone remembering to move a card.
- "Waiting on client" is an explicit, visible state with automatic reminders — the pipeline never lies about where time is lost.
- Every step links to an SOP + a short training video (20-80 keep a 26-video playlist for the build process alone). If a step can't be documented and taught, it isn't a process yet.

## 4. The Ongoing Support Loop (what you sell after launch)

One-off setup: sign-up → welcome to the support community → reputation-management pack → schedule the rhythm.

Then a **monthly/quarterly loop**, forever:

```
invoice → meeting → reports (new customers · ROI & SEO) →
document notes & actions → implement actions →
research & suggest improvements → inform & review → repeat
```

- Reports lead with 3 plain sentences; charts come after.
- Meeting notes become owned, dated action items — theirs and yours, both visible.
- Anything promised in a meeting goes on a **promise register** and is checked before the next report goes out.
- The loop is where retention lives. The build wins the client; the loop keeps them for years.

## 5. Design & Content Standards (the taste layer)

- **Structure encodes conversion**: every page answers *who is this for → why this business → proof → one clear action* in that order. One primary call-to-action per page.
- **Local-first**: suburb names in titles, headings and copy where natural; a page per priority suburb only when there's real content for it (thin suburb pages are worse than none — < 250 words means write more or don't ship the page).
- **Fees/pricing transparency** wherever the industry allows — it pre-qualifies enquiries.
- **Photography of the real team and premises** beats stock everywhere it exists; the shoot is offered early (week 3–4), not as an afterthought.
- **Bilingual as a feature, not a translation**: if the business serves a second-language community, pages are *adapted* (tone, cultural framing, locked terminology glossary), never machine-translated word-for-word — and both language versions are kept in sync from then on.
- **Accessibility & speed are acceptance criteria**, not enhancements (mobile speed and schema are on the 22-point go-live checklist).

## 6. Engagement Anti-patterns (things this philosophy explicitly avoids)

- ❌ Asking the client "what do you want your website to say?" (they hire you to know)
- ❌ Sequential design-then-content (deadlock generator)
- ❌ SEO promises before the site exists
- ❌ Unlimited revisions instead of two structured approval gates
- ❌ Reporting activity ("we posted 8 times") instead of outcomes ("6 enquiries came from Google this week")
- ❌ Compliance review at the end (rules belong at the point of writing)
- ❌ Letting "waiting on client" be invisible (chase politely, automatically, visibly)
- ❌ Jargon in any client-facing sentence

## 7. Reusing This on Other Projects

The machinery is industry-agnostic; only the **knowledge layer** changes. To port this to a different vertical, replace four documents and keep everything else:

| Versioned document | Healthcare instance | Swap for your industry |
|---|---|---|
| Design/site philosophy | practice-site page priorities, conversion patterns | your vertical's proven structure |
| SEO rules | suburb-first local strategy, money-keyword picks | your market's search behaviour |
| Compliance rulebook | AHPRA (no testimonials/guarantees) | trade licensing, finance/legal advertising rules, etc. |
| Tone & language guide | patient-and-dollar plain English (+中文 glossary) | your audience's plain language |

Keep unchanged: the 10-question intake, the PRD merge, the strategist gate, the two approval gates, the event-driven pipeline, the chase sequences, the support loop, the promise register, and the plain-language reporting formula.
